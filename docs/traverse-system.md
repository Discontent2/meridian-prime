# Meridian Prime Traverse System

> Expandable canon reference for inland Traverse operations in the Meridian Prime universe.

**Canon Status:** CONFIRMED CANON, EXPANDABLE

Large overland expeditions on Meridian Prime are called **Traverses** in official World Works / Prime Ops language. Traverses are seasonal, numbered, acronym-based operations that depart from **Prime Ops** to support extraction sites, science outposts, route proving, surveying, prospecting, and inland infrastructure work.

Traverses are dangerous, highly procedural, and culturally important. They are not casual road trips. They are moving industrial survival missions involving traverse tractors, Mods, heavy equipment operators, mechanics, Leads, Mountaineers, engineers, logistics crews, infrastructure monitors, route specialists, flagmen, Track Bikes, fuel caches, and sometimes mixed-species expertise.

**Core Rule:** Every major Traverse has an acronym, a number, a command structure, a seasonal purpose, and a place in the departure chain.

---

## Traverse Ops Terminology

### Traverse / On Traverse

**Traverse** is always the correct generic term for a major overland expedition on Meridian Prime.

Official World Works / Prime Ops documents capitalize **Traverse**.

Crews use **traverse** casually in speech, but when a crew is deployed in the field, they are said to be:

**On Traverse**

This is more than a location phrase. It describes a field condition, a work state, and a temporary culture. People behave differently On Traverse because the terrain, machinery, weather, route rules, and survival procedures become more important than ordinary Prime Ops hierarchy.

Canon note:

**Things are different On Traverse.**

### Traverse Seasons

Current operational season terms:

- **WINFLY** = spring / early-season flight and preparation window
- **Mainbody** = summer / main operational season
- **Offload** = fall / return, unloading, and drawdown season
- **Winter-Over** = winter / overwintering status or season

These terms are World Works / Meridian Prime logistics terminology. They are not references to real-world Antarctic operations.

Use them as inherited corporate field language that has become part of Traverse Ops culture.

### Route / Trail

The official planned course of a Traverse is named by destination:

- **Route to Spire**
- **Route to Prime Ops**
- **Route to [destination]**

The Route to Spire and Route to Prime Ops can refer to the same physical corridor depending on direction of travel.

Crew slang for the route is:

**The Trail**

Example usage:

- Official: “SpITE I is assigned to prove the Route to Spire.”
- Crew: “Don’t trust the Trail past the third marker.”
- Return usage: “We’re on the Route to Prime Ops now.”

### Unproven Route / Proven Route

**Unproven Route to [destination]**

A route is unproven when the seasonal path has not yet been validated for current conditions.

**Proven Route to [destination]**

A route is not considered proven until all of the following have happened:

1. A full **GPR** scan has been completed.
2. **GOS** waypoints have been confirmed or relocated.
3. The relevant Traverse I has reached its destination.
4. A **SITREP** has been submitted.

Acronyms:

- **GPR** = Ground-Penetrating Radar
- **GOS** = Ground Orientation System
- **SITREP** = Situation Report

**GOS waypoints are not treated as abstract map pins only. They are field-confirmed navigation anchors whose status must be physically or instrumentally verified because Meridian Prime terrain shifts, route conditions change, and official maps can become dangerously wrong.**

### The Path

**The Path** is not the whole route.

**The Path** is the proven passage through a crevasse field, designated by black flags.

The Path is treated seriously by crews. It is quiet, somber, and procedural. It is where joking usually drops away because a black-flagged path through a crevasse field is safe only because crews made it safe for the season.

Canon distinction:

- **The Trail** = crew slang for the larger route.
- **The Path** = the proven black-flagged passage through a crevasse field.
- **Proven Route** = official status for a route to a destination after validation criteria are met.

### “Proven Path” Sarcasm

**Proven Path** is crew sarcasm.

Crews may call suspicious sections of a route a **Proven Path** when they technically have official safe status but feel wrong in the field.

Example usage:

- “Prime Ops says it’s a Proven Path.”
- “Yeah. That’s what worries me.”

This phrase should carry grim humor and distrust of paperwork.

### Support Logic

Traverse Ops proverb:

**Traverse Ops is officially there to support science. Everyone else is there to support Traverse.**

Officially, Traverse Ops supports science, extraction, and inland operations.

In practice, nothing reaches the inland sites unless the Traverse survives.

**Supported** means a Traverse can reach its destination with the fuel, route data, machinery, crew, communications, and operational support available for that mission.

### Last Season’s Route

**Last Season’s Route to [destination]** is the term for a route used or opened in the previous season.

This term carries caution. Last Season’s Route may be useful, but it is not automatically safe this season. It must still be checked, scanned, relocated, re-flagged, and proven again if conditions require it.

Example:

- “That’s Last Season’s Route to Spire. It still needs GPR before I put a tractor on it.”

### SpITE / MITE / WWISSaRD Pronunciation and Culture

#### SpITE

**SpITE** is pronounced “spite” by crews.

Officials usually avoid the slang pronunciation and call it by its full name:

**Spire Inland Traverse Excursion**

Crew pronunciation carries field humor. The acronym is official, but the way crews say it belongs to Traverse Ops culture.

#### MITE

**MITE** is pronounced “mighty” by MITE crews.

Other traverses often pronounce it “might,” especially in joking or casual rivalry.

Calling MITE crews **mites** is disrespectful and usually appears only when tensions are rising between traverses.

MITE crews lean into the underdog pride of “mighty.” They may have the hand-me-down tractors, but they are proud of getting ugly work done.

#### WWISSaRD

**WWISSaRD** is usually pronounced “wizard.”

It may be pronounced **W-Wizard** when someone is poking fun at World Works or the science crews.

WWISSaRD crews generally embrace the ridiculous acronym because they are out there doing science and know the name is absurd.

### Command Terms

#### Lead

**Lead** is the field-shortened term for **Traverse Leader**.

The Lead is the operational commander of a Traverse.

Example:

- Official: “Traverse Leader authorization required.”
- Crew: “Ask the Lead.”

#### Mountaineer

The **Mountaineer** is the route specialist responsible for reading GPR, suggesting the path through crevasse zones, assessing terrain viability, and serving as wilderness EMT.

A Mountaineer is essential for SpITE I / MITE I route-opening operations and other dangerous terrain work.

Canon responsibilities:

- read or interpret GPR results
- assess crevasse fields
- suggest the safest path through crevasse zones
- advise the Lead on terrain viability
- identify dangerous slopes, unstable surfaces, and route hazards
- serve as wilderness EMT during field emergencies

#### HEO-IC

**HEO-IC** is official shorthand for:

**Heavy Equipment Operator in Command**

SpITE II is led by the HEO-IC.

#### HEM-IC

**HEM-IC** is official shorthand for:

**Heavy Equipment Mechanic in Command**

SpITE III is led by the HEM-IC.

#### IC

**IC** means **In Command**.

Each Traverse has one IC. Command does not split officially, though specialists such as the Mountaineer may have strong authority within their domain.

## Prime Ops

**Prime Ops** is the central operational departure hub for inland Traverses on Meridian Prime.

Prime Ops handles:

- traverse scheduling
- tractor staging
- Mod assignments
- route reports
- fuel planning
- fuel cache records
- heavy equipment deployment
- science cargo manifests
- extraction site support
- route permissions
- emergency response coordination
- official World Works recordkeeping

Prime Ops is both a practical field base and a bureaucratic control point. It decides what officially happened, what gets logged, what gets delayed, and what disappears into paperwork.

## Spire

**Spire** is the largest crystal extraction and science experiment site on Meridian Prime.

Spire is:

- the largest known crystal extraction site
- a major World Works science experiment site
- the furthest major active site from Prime Ops
- the most prestigious and dangerous inland destination
- the central objective of the SpITE traverse chain

Spire should feel like more than a mine. It is an extraction site, research site, corporate prize, and possible buried-truth location.

## SpITE Traverses

**SpITE** is an official Traverse acronym.

**SpITE = Spire Inland Traverse Excursion**

SpITE Traverses are the main seasonal Traverses to Spire. They are numbered in departure order.

Examples:

- SpITE I
- SpITE II
- SpITE III

### SpITE I

**SpITE I** is the first Spire Traverse of the season.

SpITE I is the **route-proving Traverse**.

Primary purpose:

- prove the seasonal Route to Spire
- prove the seasonal Route to Prime Ops
- inspect route infrastructure
- mark hazards
- test or confirm the path through dangerous terrain
- open the season for heavier follow-up Traverses

Command structure:

SpITE I requires both:

- a **Lead** / Traverse Leader
- a **Mountaineer**

The Lead commands the operational mission. The Mountaineer proves terrain viability, reads GPR, suggests the path through crevasse zones, and helps determine whether the route is safe enough for heavy follow-up traffic.

SpITE I is the knife-tip of the season. It is smaller, earlier, more exposed, and more likely to encounter route contradictions before heavier corporate machinery arrives.

### SpITE II

**SpITE II** is the heavy mainbody Traverse to Spire.

SpITE II departs **one month after SpITE I and MITE I depart Prime Ops**, assuming the seasonal route has been proven and marked.

SpITE II is led by the **HEO-IC**.

**HEO-IC = Heavy Equipment Operator in Command.**

Primary purpose:

- open the Route to Spire for the heavy mainbody after re-flagging and following the proven seasonal route
- move major equipment
- haul heavy cargo
- bring extraction modules
- support large-scale Spire operations
- establish or reinforce mainbody field presence
- exploit the route proven by SpITE I and MITE I

SpITE II should feel like the steel avalanche. It is the big commitment. Once it leaves Prime Ops, the season becomes much harder to stop. SpITE II is not usually mocked for following the route opened by SpITE I / MITE I; it carries major risk and responsibility.

### SpITE III

**SpITE III** is the mechanical sustainment Traverse to Spire.

SpITE III departs **two weeks after SpITE II**, unless weather, route collapse, corporate priority, or emergency conditions alter the schedule.

SpITE III is led by the **HEM-IC**.

**HEM-IC = Heavy Equipment Mechanic in Command.**

Primary purpose:

- repair support
- replacement parts
- fuel and maintenance cargo
- tractor recovery
- Mod repair
- broken equipment rescue
- route infrastructure stabilization
- keeping the Spire operation alive after the mainbody has committed

SpITE III is the “keep the beast alive” Traverse.

### SpITE IV

If SpITE IV exists in a season, it is a later follow-on Traverse that tends to inherit everyone else’s mess.

SpITE IV is often a target of traverse jokes because late-season follow-ons can receive degraded roads, worn crews, contradictory reports, delayed cargo, and damaged equipment.

## MITE Traverses

**MITE** is an official Traverse acronym.

**MITE = Meridian Inland Traverse Extension**

MITE Traverses support lesser inland extraction sites, exploration zones, surveying routes, and prospecting operations.

MITE crews are lower prestige than SpITE crews but often more adaptable, improvisational, and field-clever.

Canon inside joke:

**MITE crews get the hand-me-down traverse tractors.**

Common MITE jokes may include:

- “SpITE gets the iron. MITE gets what still starts.”
- “MITE tractors are previously loved and structurally cursed.”
- “SpITE gets the route report. MITE gets the rocks that tried to eat the tractor.”

MITE I and SpITE IV are often the butt of traverse jokes, and most crews attached to those runs accept the humor as part of field culture unless the joke becomes genuinely disrespectful.

### MITE I

**MITE I** departs Prime Ops at the same time as **SpITE I**.

SpITE I and MITE I begin the season as a paired route-opening operation.

They travel together through the most dangerous shared early route segment: **SZ 275-300 / The Claw**.

MITE I helps **Break-in the Claw** before branching away.

After the seasonal path through SZ 275-300 / The Claw is proven, opened, reinforced, and marked:

- SpITE I continues toward Spire.
- MITE I branches away toward extension routes for lesser extraction sites, exploration, surveying, and prospecting.

MITE I may carry its own objectives, but its first responsibility is to help open the dangerous shared inland passage for the season.

## WWISSaRD Traverses

**WWISSaRD** is an official World Works science Traverse acronym.

**WWISSaRD = World Works Inland Science Support and Research Development**

WWISSaRD Traverses support science outposts, experimental sites, research stations, survey camps, and specialized field studies across Meridian Prime.

They may be numbered depending on how many scientific research operations are active that season.

Examples:

- WWISSaRD I
- WWISSaRD II
- WWISSaRD III

WWISSaRD Traverses are cleaner on paper than they are in the field. They often go to places extraction crews avoid, ignore, or consider too strange to prioritize.

Tone note: **WWISSaRD** should feel very World Works: overbuilt, committee-made, self-important, and faintly ridiculous. Field crews may mock it, but the acronym is official.

## Provisional Seasonal Departure Chain

Current standard seasonal logic:

1. **SpITE I and MITE I depart Prime Ops together.**
2. They jointly Break-in the Claw through **SZ 275-300 / The Claw**.
3. After The Claw is proven, SpITE I continues toward Spire.
4. MITE I branches into extension routes for lesser extraction sites, exploration, surveying, and prospecting.
5. **SpITE II departs one month after SpITE I / MITE I**, carrying the heavy mainbody toward Spire.
6. **SpITE III departs two weeks after SpITE II**, led by the HEM-IC, carrying sustainment, repair, and recovery capability.
7. **WWISSaRD Traverses depart later or as scheduled**, depending on science priorities, seasonal route status, and World Works research demand.

Guardrail: this departure chain is the current standard seasonal logic, but specific seasons may vary due to weather, route collapse, corporate pressure, emergency extraction orders, lost crews, scientific urgency, or concealed World Works motives.

## Traverse Tractor Naming, Numbering, and Types

Traverse tractors are identified by simple numbers.

Examples:

- 289
- 117
- 42

The tractor’s number is also its usual call sign.

### MITE Tractor Naming

MITE tractors are older and carry both numbers and names.

MITE tractor names are usually sarcastic defect-names based on the machine’s specific flaw, chronic failure, or personality problem.

Examples:

- Tight Tracks
- Cold Start
- Hard Left
- Lazy Blade
- No Reverse
- Soft Brake
- Three Tries
- Leaky Saint

MITE tractor names are stenciled directly onto the machine.

Canon note:

**Machines get insulted. Cracks get respected.**

MITE crews mock their machines because they depend on them. The insult-name is a form of familiarity, superstition, frustration, and affection.

### Other Traverse Tractor Names

SpITE, WWISSaRD, and other higher-priority Traverse crews may privately name tractors among themselves, but these names are usually not painted on the machines.

They remain unofficial crew speech.

### Lead Tractor Call Sign

The Lead tractor uses a call sign based on the Traverse name plus tractor number.

Written shorthand examples:

- SpITE I-04
- MITE I-22
- WWISSaRD II-71

Radio speech may render this more clearly as:

- “SpITE One Lead Zero-Four”
- “MITE One Lead Twenty-Two”

Ordinary follow tractors are usually called by number.

Tractors generally pull modules, while Lead tractors usually carry their specialized equipment.

Official term:

**Module**

Crew term:

**Mod**

Each follow tractor pulls at least one multifunctional Mod and usually a fuel bladder behind the Mod.

Common Traverse loads include:

- fuel bladders
- science support equipment
- solar generator
- reefer unit / cold storage
- berthing / kitchen / comms Mod
- mechanic storage unit
- Magic Carpet recovery system

Mods may combine functions rather than existing as single-purpose boxes.

Examples:

- berthing / kitchen / comms
- mechanic storage / repair support

### Worst Tractor Failure

The most feared tractor failure is not one specific mechanical category.

The worst failure is:

**the one the mechanic cannot fix in the field.**

When the mechanic cannot fix a tractor On Traverse, crew may have to ride along in the berthing Mod, redistribute loads, tow the dead machine, abandon nonessential cargo, or mark equipment for recovery.

## Recovery Sled / Magic Carpet / Tool

Official term:

**Recovery Sled**

Crew term:

**Magic Carpet**

The Magic Carpet is a large sheet of high molecular weight plastic used to drag broken equipment.

Standard field image:

- approximately 100 feet long
- approximately 40 feet wide
- black high molecular weight plastic sheet
- used to tow damaged or immobilized equipment
- attached to a Tool

### Tool

The **Tool** is the tow bar only.

The Tool includes:

- skis
- D-rings
- tow hardware

The Tool is not the Magic Carpet itself. The Tool connects the towing machine to the Recovery Sled / Magic Carpet system.

### Tool and Bladder Numbering

Tools, fuel bladders, and Magic Carpets are numbered for internal Traverse Ops logistics, not formal World Works registry.

This numbering is usually a **Winter-Over job**.

Winter-Over crews inventory, number, patch, sticker, repair, and argue over the equipment that will keep Traverses alive during WINFLY and Mainbody.

Color canon:

- Tools are red.
- Fuel bladders are black.
- HMW sheets / Magic Carpets are black.

## Track Bikes

**Track Bike** is the official and crew term.

Track Bikes are standard Traverse Ops equipment.

Each Traverse team carries **two Track Bikes** for redundancy.

Track Bikes are:

- electric
- numbered
- equipped with deployable solar panel arrays
- unable to travel while the panel array is deployed
- maintained by the team mechanic
- stored on the Magic Carpet Tool tongue

Track Bikes generally do not need call signs because they are used locally around the Traverse, especially during flag work, route checks, and return-trip maintenance.

### Track Bike Duty

Track Bike duty is not treated as an insult.

Green traversers may be assigned alternate roles, and if a tractor is down, personnel from that tractor may take over daily flag replacement or route-maintenance sections by Track Bike.

Common use cases:

- flag replacement
- checking missing markers
- short local route inspection
- return-trip Path maintenance
- moving around safe zones or camped Traverse layouts

## Named Cracks

**Named Cracks** is the term for known recurring cracks.

Named Cracks are recurring cracks or crevasse hazards known by crews through memorial field culture.

They are often named after the first or worst person known to have died crossing them.

Named Crack memorial names are preserved by word of mouth, not official public maps.

Prime Ops knows about Named Cracks and catalogs them, but associated deaths are not recorded openly. Families may be paid off or official records softened. World Works dislikes Named Cracks because they preserve fatality memory outside approved paperwork, but Prime Ops still catalogs them because the information is operationally useful.

Canon distinction:

- World Works records the hazard.
- Traverse Ops remembers the dead.

### NC Numbering

Named Cracks receive NC numbers in official or internal Traverse Ops cataloging.

Format example:

**NC-S+285**

Meaning:

- **NC** = Named Crack
- **S** = Route to Spire
- **+285** = mile 285 toward Spire

Directional rule:

- Toward Spire is always **plus**.
- Toward Prime Ops is always **minus**.

Most Trails branch from the Route to Spire. When a branch becomes its own route, it receives its own Route to designation.

Example:

**NC-WC1+30**

Meaning:

- Named Crack
- Route to WWISSaRD Camp 1
- mile 30 on that route

Canon route shorthand:

- **S** = Route to Spire
- **WC1** = Route to WWISSaRD Camp 1
- **MX1** = MITE Exploration Route 1

Future routes may add additional codes.

## Trail Flags and Route Marking

Most Trail flags use Meridian Prime bamboo poles with cloth flags.

Trail flags are placed roughly every mile, depending on terrain, weather, urgency, and who is on the Track Bike that day.

Crew truth:

**The Trail is flagged by procedure and maintained by whoever drew Track Bike duty.**

### Special Markers

Certain locations use larger 4x4 markers with solar-powered red rotating lights.

Special marker locations include:

- Prime Date Line
- Trail forks
- crevasse field entrances
- major route transitions
- important mile markers

Special markers are also mile markers, so their numbers depend on location.

### Prime Date Line

**Prime Date Line** is the Meridian Prime / World Works term.

Do not use “International Date Line” for canon except as rejected or obsolete terminology.

### Flag Colors

Flag colors indicate the primary route function.

#### Green Flags

Green flags mark the primary Route to / from Spire.

#### Blue Flags

Blue flags mark WWISSaRD routes.

Science route identity remains blue. No flag color changes are made for science route function once established.

#### Red Flags

Red flags mark MITE routes, including exploration routes.

All MITE routes are red whether they have a destination or not, until taken over by another department.

If a MITE route becomes useful enough to be taken over, MITE receives funding for another season. MITE crews generally do not resent this because their mission was successful.

If a route color changes, the physical flag change is done by the Traverse team **flagman of the day**.

#### Black Flags

Black flags mark The Path through a crevasse field.

Black flags are the most serious flag type.

### Flagman

**Flagman** is the field role term.

The flagman of the day may replace, adjust, or physically change flags under Traverse authority.

Flagman duties may fall to:

- Mountaineer on return trip
- green traversers assigned alternate duty
- crew from a down tractor
- whoever is assigned route-flag work that day

The term is field language and may be used regardless of gender unless future canon revises it.

## SZ 275-300 / The Claw / Aeon’s Claw

The dangerous crevasse field between Prime Ops and Spire has multiple names.

**Official World Works / Prime Ops Designation:** SZ 275-300

**Meaning:** Shear Zone, Mile Marker 275 to 300

**Official Field Name:** The Shear Zone

**Crew / Field Term:** The Claw or Claw

**Aeonolacertian-Derived Term:** Aeon’s Claw

**Aeon’s Claw** is probably a bastardized Meridian translation of an older Aeonolacertian phrase. The exact original Aeonolacertian term remains open for future development.

Term hierarchy:

- **SZ 275-300** = official route / map designation
- **The Shear Zone** = official or semi-official field name
- **The Claw / Claw** = Traverse Ops crew term
- **Aeon’s Claw** = Aeonolacertian-derived / translated cultural name

The Claw is a known seasonal hazard. It contains crevasses, unstable lips, shifting ice/crystal/stone fields, and route conditions that can change between seasons.

SpITE I and MITE I both carry explosives because safe seasonal passage sometimes requires controlled crevasse reduction.

High-level fictional procedure:

**The crews locate the narrowest viable crossing, drill a sanctioned control pattern, collapse unstable material under controlled conditions, then pack, bridge, fill, reinforce, and mark the crossing for the season.**

This procedure is fictional, high-level, and story-facing. It should not be expanded into a real-world explosives manual.

Possible in-world slang or procedural terms:

- cutting the tooth
- Break-in the Claw
- claw-breaking
- seasonal collapse work
- controlled crevasse reduction
- making the fill
- opening the Claw

### Break-in the Claw

**Break-in the Claw** is the common field phrase for opening, proving, marking, and making passable the seasonal route through SZ 275-300 / The Claw.

SpITE I and MITE I **Break-in the Claw** at the start of the season.

Use **Break-in the Claw** as the common field phrase, not “breaking the claw.”

The phrase may include controlled crevasse reduction, GPR scanning, GOS waypoint confirmation or relocation, black-flag Path designation, fill work, bridge work, reinforcement, and route marking.

Keep all explosive and crevasse references fictional, high-level, and story-facing. Do not expand this into real-world technical procedure.

## The Path and Black Flag Doctrine

The Path is the proven black-flagged passage through a crevasse field.

Known crevasse fields are well flagged at their boundaries by crossed bamboo black flags. Plywood signage designates the entrance.

At approved bridge crossings, crossed black flags appear on each side of the crossing.

Crossed black flags use plywood spray-painted signage with skull-and-crossbones imagery.

Prime Ops does not officially acknowledge skull-and-crossbones signage. It ignores photos of such signage and does not address it in formal reports.

There is no special crew nickname for the crossed black-flag gate. Crews simply refer to entering or exiting the crevasse field or Path.

### Black Flag Side Rule

The black-flag side rule is formal Traverse Ops doctrine and applies to all crevasse fields.

On the Route to Spire:

**black flags are on your left.**

On the Route to Prime Ops:

**black flags are on your right.**

The Path is one lane. Getting the black-flag side rule wrong can mean the crew is traveling the wrong side, wrong direction, wrong Path, or wrong crossing.

Messing this up can get a crew member removed from future Traverse work, or killed.

Canon reminder phrase before entering a crevasse field:

- “Alright, we are entering the Shear Zone. Flags on your left.”
- “Alright, we are entering the Shear Zone. Flags on your right.”

Direction depends on travel.

### Missing Black Flags

Missing black flags are considered the worst flag failure.

Crew callout:

**“Flags missing between markers [marker number] and [marker number].”**

On The Path, when flags are missing:

**“Flags missing. Follow the Lead.”**

Prime Ops euphemism:

**Special Flagging Operations**

A missing black flag incident can be treated as **dereliction of duty** for the Traverse IC if the failure results from negligence.

Prime Ops may not publicly call out individual failures, but personnel responsible may simply not be called back for the next season.

After the Claw is proven, SpITE I continues to Spire while MITE I branches into extension routes for exploration, surveying, prospecting, and lesser-site support. SpITE II departs one month later with the heavy mainbody, trusting the opened seasonal road.**

This paired opener is a major seasonal event.

It involves:

- route proving
- GPR scanning

- crevasse work
- terrain assessment
- Mountaineer decisions
- explosive-certified route crews
- tractor testing
- infrastructure monitoring
- route marking

- SpITE I goes toward the official prize.

## Land Growth / Crystal Growth

Crew phrase:

**The land grew.**

Science / official terminology:

**crystal growth**

Operational report term:

**Growth Event**

A Growth Event may be logged as:

**Section between mile markers [X] and [Y] experienced a Growth Event.**

Land growth can affect:

- flags
- poles
- markers
- equipment
- Trail segments
- Path segments
- parked machines
- route viability

The land can grow a route to unusability.

Crews usually use “the land grew” as field speech, but Science may become involved when growth exceeds normal expectations or becomes linked to larger lunar-cycle patterns.

### Growth Events and Lunar Cycles

Crystal Growth Events usually have no direct warning.

They mostly depend on lunar cycles.

This remains open for future development, especially regarding WWISSaRD research, lunar prediction, and whether World Works hides or manipulates growth data.

### Pressure Ridges

The Traverse team will reopen pressure ridges upon discovery.

General scale:

- 10-foot pressure ridges may take a few days to clear.
- 20-foot pressure ridges may take about a week to clear.
- Nothing over 30 feet has been recorded on the Route to Spire.

Crystal pressure ridges can get worse through the season, but passing Traverses clip them as they travel.

Grooming teams at camps and stations use drags for pressure ridge maintenance.

Traverse tractors use front-end multidirectional blades.

## Crystal Storms

Crystal storms are distinct from slow crystal growth.

Crystal growth is usually slow.

Crystal storms can bury whole Traverse teams if they do not keep moving.

No one can cross The Claw or any crevasse field during a crystal storm.

This rule cannot be overridden by the Lead or Traverse IC.

If anyone has crossed a crevasse field during a crystal storm and survived, they do not talk about it. Whether it has happened remains unknown.

### Crystal Storm Appearance

Crystal storms may include:

- blowing crystal dust
- vertical glittering shards
- whiteout-like opacity
- red-light reflections
- electrical glow
- nacreous luminescent noctilucent dark clouds on the horizon

First warning sign:

**nacreous, luminescent, noctilucent dark clouds on the horizon getting bigger / closer by the hour.**

### Navigation During Crystal Storms

Crystal storms do not directly interfere with GPR or GOS.

The larger navigation problem is insufficient satellite coverage.

GOS may become less reliable because there are not enough satellites or support signals, not because the storm itself scrambles the system.

## Circle Camp / Storm Hold

Correct term:

**Circle Camp**

Capitalization does not matter in field speech.

Official / Prime Ops term:

**Storm Hold**

Crew / Traverse Ops term:

**Circle Camp**

When a Traverse approaches The Claw or another crevasse field at the beginning of a crystal storm, it cannot enter the crevasse field.

Instead, it holds outside the crevasse field in a known safe zone associated with a fuel cache.

The Traverse drives in slow, approximately 5-mile-diameter circles for about 10 hours, then stops, refuels, eats dinner, and sleeps.

In the morning, the crew digs itself out or “breaks free,” then repeats the process until the storm passes.

General duration:

- bad storm = about 3 days
- ten-year storm = about 1 week

Canon phrase:

**Circle Camp is not retreat. Circle Camp is survival.**

### Safe Zones and Fuel Caches

Safe zones are known by the fuel cache.

Fuel caches are known by mile marker.

There is a fuel cache on each side of a crevasse field.

Fuel caches consist of buried or berm-stored bladders or tanks, usually totaling around 10,000 gallons across three to five bladders / tanks.

Caches are stored on berms.

Traverse Ops maintains and refuels the caches.

Fuel caches make Circle Camp possible.

If a fuel cache is missing, empty, buried, contaminated, or misreported, the situation becomes an immediate major hazard.

## Stop Ops

Emergency halt phrase:

**“Stop ops, stop ops, stop ops.”**

This is the phrase a Lead never wants to hear from a Mountaineer while On The Path.

It indicates an immediate halt or suspension of operations due to route danger, Path uncertainty, missing flags, terrain instability, GPR concern, or other urgent field hazard.

“Stop ops” can become one of the most frightening phrases in Traverse Ops because it means the normal procedure has failed and the crew must rely on judgment before moving again.

Open question for future development:

**Who besides the Mountaineer has authority to call “stop ops, stop ops, stop ops,” and what happens if someone calls it frivolously?**

## Traverse Crew Roles

### Traverse Leader / Lead

The operational commander of a Traverse. Responsible for mission authority, crew discipline, safety calls, route decisions, stop/go calls, communication with Prime Ops, and whether to continue, retreat, or abandon cargo.

In field speech, this role is usually shortened to **Lead**.

### Mountaineer

A route specialist responsible for reading or interpreting GPR, assessing crevasse fields, suggesting the safest path through crevasse zones, advising the Lead on terrain viability, identifying dangerous slopes or unstable surfaces, and serving as wilderness EMT during field emergencies.

The Mountaineer is essential for SpITE I / MITE I route-opening operations and other dangerous terrain work.

### HEO

**Heavy Equipment Operator.**

Operates traverse tractors, loaders, rigs, winches, anchor vehicles, heavy Mods, and specialized route-opening equipment. HEOs are especially central to SpITE II and heavy mainbody operations.

### HEO-IC

**Heavy Equipment Operator in Command.**

SpITE II is led by the HEO-IC.

### HEM

**Heavy Equipment Mechanic.**

Responsible for major mechanical repair, tractor survival, Mod repair, recovery operations, field fabrication, parts management, and keeping the Traverse alive when machinery fails. HEMs are central to SpITE III.

### HEM-IC

**Heavy Equipment Mechanic in Command.**

SpITE III is led by the HEM-IC.

### IC

**In Command.**

Each Traverse has one IC. Command does not split officially, though specialists such as the Mountaineer may have strong authority within their domain.

### Mechanic

Handles general repairs, fabrication, improvised fixes, Mod systems, engine work, heater systems, field tools, Track Bikes, and day-to-day machine survival. Often overlaps culturally with Dieselwright logic.

### Engineer / Logistics Specialist

Manages cargo math, fuel, manifests, route timing, supply priority, bridge/fill calculations, risk margins, and compatibility between mission plans and physical reality.

### Module-Attached Mech / Infrastructure Monitor

A specialized role involving a crew member or operator who attaches to, rides with, or interfaces directly with a traverse Mod during travel. This role monitors traverse infrastructure, communications, tension, heat, old route systems, coupling behavior, dead channels, corrupted terminals, and Mod health.

This role may later connect strongly to Antisapien or mixed-species crew design, especially characters capable of reading old systems, exposed wires, or buried infrastructure.

### Explosive-Certified Route Crew

A specialized route-opening crew used in The Claw and other unstable terrain. They perform fictional controlled crevasse reduction and seasonal route-opening work under Traverse authority.

## Artist and Adaptation Rules

- Traverses should look like moving industrial survival systems, not simple convoys.
- Traverse tractors should feel massive, practical, repaired, and lived-in.
- MITE tractors should look older, patched, named, and defect-proud.
- SpITE tractors should generally look newer, cleaner, numbered, and higher-priority.
- Lead tractors should be visually distinct and GPR-capable.
- Mods should look multifunctional, practical, heated, and lived-in.
- Magic Carpets / Recovery Sleds should look like massive black HMW sheets attached through red Tool tow bars.
- Tools should be red.
- Fuel bladders should be black.
- Track Bikes should look like practical electric track vehicles with deployable solar arrays.
- Named Cracks should feel memorial and dangerous, not funny.
- MITE tractor names should feel funny, practical, and affectionate through insult.
- Trail flags should be mostly bamboo poles with cloth flags.
- Special 4x4 markers with red rotating solar lights should stand out at major route features.
- Black flags and skull-crossing signage should feel serious and field-made, not theatrical.
- SZ 275-300 / The Claw should visually feel like a threshold wound in the landscape.
- The Path through a crevasse field should be visibly black-flagged, quiet, one-lane, and procedural.
- Crevasse work should be shown as dangerous fictional route work, not as a real technical guide.
- Circle Camp should look like a Traverse slowly grinding circles outside a forbidden crevasse field under an approaching crystal storm.
- Crystal storms should feel beautiful, lethal, nacreous, luminescent, and procedurally terrifying.
- Mixed crews should be designed around practical expertise, not token presence.
- Prime Ops should feel both useful and controlling.
- Spire should feel distant, prestigious, and ominous.
