# Chapter 01: Cold Start - Rough Prose Draft

> Rough prose test pass for Chapter 1. This is draft material, not final manuscript canon.

**Draft Status:** ROUGH DRAFT, EXPANDABLE

---

Red Umbrielor complained before dawn.

Tenet Nocsid III stood beside the machine with one hand on cold steel and waited for the MD-8 system to finish lying to itself.

The old Quadtrack ticked, clicked, muttered, and flashed a warning amber across the side diagnostic strip. Nothing catastrophic. Catastrophe had a different sound. Catastrophe had teeth in it. This was ordinary morning hatred from an old machine with a long memory and a deep belief that no civilized engine should be asked to wake before the yard lights went white.

Tenet kept his glove pressed against the scarred red flank below the cab ladder. The paint had been patched so many times it no longer matched itself. Some panels were oxblood. Some were rust-red. Some were the dull brown of primer that had given up waiting for finish coat. The number **289** sat stenciled under a skin of frost, half rubbed away where cargo straps had worried at it season after season.

Red Umbrielor was awake before the sun.

So was NCI.

The staging yard spread around him in dark blocks and moving lights. Mods sat on their skids like sleeping buildings. Fuel crews walked the lanes with their collars up and their voices low. Somebody had backed a loader too close to a stack of tool crates and was now being educated by three mechanics who all seemed to be using the same swear words in different technical disciplines.

Private work never looked as clean as Prime Ops wanted it to look. That was part of its charm and most of its value.

Red Umbrielor coughed again.

The diagnostic strip flickered.

MD-8 PRESSURE UNEVEN.

Tenet watched it for three breaths.

The warning cleared.

“Yeah,” he said. “Thought so.”

The machine answered with a long internal whine as the fuel warmer caught up. Its tracks settled a fraction into the frozen yard grit. Somewhere under the engine cowling, a pump remembered its job and stopped complaining about it.

He climbed the ladder and shouldered into the cab.

The defrost had quit sometime in the night, because old machines believed in timing.

The inside of the glass had gone white at the edges. The forward view was a cloudy smear of yard lights and black shapes. Tenet thumbed the defrost toggle twice, listened, then gave the dash a look he had learned from Rob Holliday.

The dash did not apologize.

“Fine.”

He reached under the auxiliary panel and kicked on the turkey roaster.

The little heater came alive with a smell like hot dust, old wire, and bad decisions that had somehow passed inspection. It rattled inside its bolted cage, glowed a dull orange, and began pushing warm air at the windshield with the enthusiasm of a bar bathroom hand dryer trying to save a marriage.

Tenet waited until the cab turned from murder-cold to merely insulting. Then he peeled out of his outer shell, shook frost off the shoulders, and hung the layer behind the seat. His breath still fogged in front of him, but the windshield began to clear in a half-moon where the turkey roaster aimed its stubborn little heart.

The passenger seat stayed empty.

It had stayed empty a long time now.

There was no duffel wedged against the footwell. No salmon sleeve showing under a thermal overcoat. No warm voice saying the defrost was bullshit, the yard was bullshit, the schedule was bullshit, and the only honest thing at dawn was coffee because coffee made no promises it could not keep.

Tenet looked away before the thought could become a memory with edges.

He leaned down and checked the under-seat compartment.

Closed.

Locked.

Still there.

Grandfather Ten’s pistol did not rattle. It never did. Wrapped in oilcloth, tucked inside the compartment beneath the driver’s seat, it rode with the machine like a second heart nobody talked about. Rob had given it to him at The House Ten Built and told him he still had time.

Tenet had not known what that meant.

He still did not.

The cab heater clicked louder. The glass cleared another inch.

Outside, the yard kept moving.

NCI did not wake all at once. It came online in grudges. One crew at the fuel racks. One at the Mod couplings. One arguing over whether a recovery tool had been numbered by a sober person or a Winter-Over philosopher. Field lamps caught on frost, pipe steam, old red paint, black fuel bladders, and the boxy silhouettes of machines that had all survived at least one thing they should not have.

The Family, people called NCI when they were being sentimental, drunk, angry, or all three.

The Company, they called it when invoices were late.

Tenet used both, depending on who had touched his equipment last.

A figure crossed the yard between two parked Mods, moving fast enough to look irritated and slow enough to make sure everyone saw it. She had a dispatch slate tucked against her ribs, a hardcopy packet under one arm, and a thermal cap pulled low over hair that had been cut short with either intention or impatience.

Lindy Nocsid walked like weather with boots on.

Tenet watched her come through the clearing smear of windshield.

She did not wave.

Lindy considered waving an admission that people were far away from each other. She preferred to arrive like a problem that had already found your address.

She hit the side step with one boot and hauled herself up far enough to lean into the open cab door.

“Your defrost still dead?”

“Resting.”

“Runs in the family.”

Tenet looked at her.

Lindy looked back.

She had the Nocsid eyes, though she hated hearing that. Tired gray-green under yard lights, sharp enough to cut paper, bruised enough to know paper cut back. There were people who mistook Lindy for fragile because she was small and quick and too often hollow-eyed before sunrise. Those people usually learned better. Some learned politely. Some required witnesses.

She sniffed the cab air.

“Turkey roaster?”

“Until the defrost remembers the contract.”

“Defrost never signed. Defrost had legal review.”

She shoved the packet at him.

Tenet took it.

The hardcopy was sealed in a field sleeve, edges stiff against the cold. The digital twin blinked onto his dash a second later, delivered through the NCI dispatch channel with a cheerful chime that belonged to some office programmer who had never watched a tractor sink to its belly in living crystal.

MITE II PROPOSED ROUTE ACCESS / SURVEYING AND PROOFING PACKAGE.

NCI INTERNAL FIELD COPY.

CONTRACT DESIGNATION: HEO-IC / FIELD LEAD.

He looked at that line first because it was his line. Not Prime Ops Lead. Not official Traverse Leader. Not anything that would make a clerk salute or a lawyer sweat. NCI MITE II HEO-IC by contract designation, field lead because somebody had to be responsible when the paper stopped mattering and the land started speaking.

Private work loved careful titles.

Careful titles kept knives sheathed until everyone was too far out to complain.

“What did they change?” Tenet asked.

“Good morning to you too.”

“It was morning before you opened the door.”

“Barely. And I improved it.”

“You brought paperwork.”

“I said improved. I didn’t say saved.”

Lindy climbed another step and braced one shoulder against the cab frame. She did not come all the way in. She liked thresholds. Doorways, dispatch windows, bad decisions. Places where a person could still pretend they had not entered.

“They pushed the digital packet at 0310,” she said. “Hardcopy printed at 0328. Route lease is attached. Equipment manifest updated. Mod train stayed mostly the same, unless you count somebody deciding your reefer support line needed a redundant coupler.”

“It did.”

“I know. I added it before they realized they wanted it.”

Tenet flipped through the field copy. The paper was real paper, heavy and treated, because digital files froze, corrupted, desynced, vanished, or became theological arguments between devices at exactly the worst possible mile marker. Every field operator he trusted liked hardcopy. Every office that wanted deniability liked updates.

The mission read clean.

Too clean was not the same as good.

MITE II. NCI-run. Private survey and route-proofing work under leased proposed route access. Equipment to verify surface viability, assess growth interference, and mark preliminary extension corridors where authorized.

Nothing in the first page snarled.

That was the first thing Tenet disliked.

He thumbed to the authorization chain.

NCI request logged.

Lease reviewed.

Route access provisionally granted.

World Works concurrence attached.

The turkey roaster rattled. The windshield cleared enough for Lindy’s reflection to appear faintly over the text, her face bent into the same expression she used when a crew member said, technically, the tool had not been lost.

Tenet read the final approval line.

HORUS SKELTON.

WORLD WORKS DIRECTOR OF TRAVERSE OPERATIONS.

His eyes stayed there.

The cab seemed to get quieter, though nothing had stopped moving.

“What the—”

Lindy did not ask what.

That was how he knew she had seen it.

“Yeah,” she said. “Saw that.”

Tenet looked up from the packet.

Lindy’s mouth had gone flat. Not guilty. Not innocent. Lindy had a whole range between those two countries and lived in most of it rent-free.

“Horus signed a MITE survey lease?”

“Approved.”

“That is not better.”

“Didn’t say it was.”

He looked back at the line. Director-level approval did not make the job illegal. It made the job insulated. A small private route-proofing lease did not need Horus Skelton’s personal hand on it unless somebody wanted everyone below him to stop asking why it moved so cleanly.

Dirty paperwork had fingerprints.

Clean paperwork wore gloves.

“Who pushed it?” Tenet asked.

Lindy tapped the digital slate against her thumb. A nervous habit she would deny under oath, family, and threat of cake.

“Came through proper.”

“That was not the question.”

“No,” she said. “It was the question you ask when you want me to say a name I don’t have.”

“You have something.”

“I have a packet.”

“You have a face.”

“I have several. Comes with the job.”

Tenet waited.

Lindy hated waiting more than she hated being pushed, which was why he did not push. The yard noise filled the gap: couplings clanking, a fuel handler calling numbers, someone laughing too loudly near the east rack, Red Umbrielor’s heater chewing its own little song into the cold.

Finally Lindy exhaled.

“It came fast,” she said. “Too fast for the number of departments pretending they read it.”

“There it is.”

“Don’t make that your satisfied face. Nobody likes that face.”

“Nobody likes any of my faces.”

“Rob did.”

The name landed between them and steamed there.

Lindy looked away first, out across the yard where Red Umbrielor’s front blade sat black against the frost. Her jaw tightened like she had bitten down on something old.

“Rob liked defective equipment,” she said. “Explains half this yard.”

Tenet let her have the dodge.

He had his own.

The passenger seat stayed empty.

The turkey roaster clicked.

He closed the field copy, then opened it again because closing it had not made Horus’s name disappear.

“What’s the destination label?” he asked.

“Proposed extension corridor.”

“Cute.”

“Very. Somebody put shoes on it and taught it to dance.”

“That specific?”

“That unspecific.”

Tenet scrolled the digital overlay. The route data was clipped close, permissioned in layers, field-readable but not generous. Enough to drive. Not enough to understand why anyone wanted him to.

He had seen bad packets before. Sloppy ones. Rushed ones. Underfunded ones. Packets built by committees that thought fuel was a mood and terrain was a suggestion.

This was not that.

This had been scrubbed until it shone.

A little private survey with a director’s blessing, a vague destination label, and enough redundant coupling to keep a reefer line happy through weather nobody had mentioned.

The work might still be honest.

The job was not.

There was a difference, and Tenet had made a living in that crack.

Lindy leaned farther in, lowering her voice even though the nearest crew was two lanes away and busy insulting a hydraulic line.

“You can kick it back.”

Tenet almost laughed.

“Can I?”

“I can lose it for ten minutes.”

“Heroic.”

“Fifteen if the printer jams tragically.”

“And after fifteen?”

“After fifteen, somebody asks why Red Umbrielor’s still sulking in the yard and why the field lead is afraid of paper.”

“I’m not afraid of paper.”

“Good. This paper would hate the satisfaction.”

He looked past her, through the clearing glass, to the Mod line hitched behind his machine. Berthing, kitchen, comms. Mechanic storage. Survey support. Fuel bladder. The pieces of a moving life, arranged in the dark by people who trusted him to notice what would kill them before it got bored and tried.

He had a crew.

He had a machine.

He had a contract.

He had his sister standing in his cab doorway with a packet she did not trust and a face that said she had delivered worse things and survived them.

He had a dead man’s pistol under his seat.

Honest work, Tenet had learned, was not work untouched by lies. It was work you did straight anyway, with both eyes open and one hand near the brake.

He signed the packet.

Lindy watched the stylus move.

“That your final answer?”

“No,” Tenet said. “That’s my opening argument.”

For half a second, Lindy smiled. It did not soften her. It made the bruises behind her eyes look older.

Then she took the hardcopy back long enough to stamp the local receipt and hand him the field sleeve.

“Try not to die on a leased route,” she said.

“Try not to dispatch me into one.”

“Runs in the family.”

This time neither of them smiled.

Tenet secured the packet in the side holder, below the dash and above the compartment he did not look at again. He shut the cab door. Lindy dropped from the step, boots hitting frozen grit, and slapped the side of Red Umbrielor once as she moved away.

The old machine answered with a low vibration through the frame.

Tenet settled into the seat. The windshield had cleared enough to show the yard in strips: lamps, vapor, moving bodies, the first pale bruise of morning behind the storage rigs. He keyed the internal channel and listened to the Mod train answer one system at a time.

Berthing heat: green.

Kitchen power: green.

Comms: green with attitude.

Mechanic storage: green.

Survey support: green.

Fuel bladder pressure: green.

Defrost: still full of lies.

The turkey roaster hummed like it knew it was indispensable.

Tenet looked once at the route packet. Horus Skelton’s name sat inside it, clean and black and heavy.

Then he looked forward.

“Red Umbrielor, MITE Two field lead,” he said into the channel. “Cold start complete. Rolling when yard gives us teeth.”

Lindy’s voice came back through dispatch, crisp under the static.

“Yard gives teeth. Try biting the road this time, not my schedule.”

“Your schedule started it.”

“Everything starts it. Move.”

Tenet eased Red Umbrielor into gear.

The old Quadtrack shifted its weight, complained once more for legal purposes, then began to roll.

The job was legal.

The machine ran.

The grief stayed quiet.

The wrongness came with them.
